What is a Mole?

A mole is the SI unit for the amount of substance. It contains exactly 6.022 × 10²³ elementary entities — atoms, molecules, or ions.

Avogadro's Number
Nₐ = 6.022 × 10²³ mol⁻¹
Number of particles in one mole of any substance. Named after Amedeo Avogadro.
💡 Easy to Remember

Just like 1 dozen = 12 items, 1 mole = 6.022 × 10²³ items. It's a counting unit for atoms and molecules!

Mole Concept — Key Connections

Molar Mass

Mass of one mole of a substance. Unit: g/mol. Equal to atomic/molecular weight numerically.

SubstanceFormulaMolar Mass (g/mol)
HydrogenH₂2
OxygenO₂32
WaterH₂O18
Carbon DioxideCO₂44
Sodium ChlorideNaCl58.5
GlucoseC₆H₁₂O₆180

Key Formulas

Number of Moles

n = mass (g) / Molar Mass (g/mol)

Number of Particles

N = n × Nₐ = (m / M) × 6.022 × 10²³

Molar Volume at STP

1 mole of any gas = 22.4 L at STP (0°C, 1 atm)

Percentage Composition

% mass = (mass of element in 1 mol / Molar Mass) × 100
Example – Water (H₂O)
% H = (2/18) × 100 = 11.11%
% O = (16/18) × 100 = 88.89%

Empirical vs Molecular Formula

Empirical Formula
Simplest whole number ratio of atoms
Glucose (C₆H₁₂O₆) → Empirical = CH₂O
Molecular Formula
Actual number of atoms in one molecule
Mol. Formula = n × Empirical, where n = Molar Mass / EF Mass
